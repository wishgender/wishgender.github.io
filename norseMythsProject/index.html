Neil Gaiman’s Norse Mythology

  • The god of war
  • The highest and oldest of the gods
  • His father was Bor (son of Buri, ancestor of the gods), and his mother was Bestla, whose brother was Mimir
  • Has two brothers: Vili and Ve, with whom he killed the primordial giant Ymir, allowing the world to form
  • Created humans with Vili and Ve, breathing life into them
  • Seeks wisdom at any cost: sacrificed an eye for wisdom; hung from the world-tree, Yggdrasil, for nine nights, during which he did not eat or drink anything. This ordeal allowed him to see the runes and understand magic.
  • Travels the world in search of wisdom, disguised as a tall man wearing a cloak and a hat
  • Accompanied by two ravens, Huginn ("thought") and Muninn ("memory"), who fly around the world to bring him information
  • Can see everything from atop his throne at Hlidskjalf
  • Drank all of the Mead of Poetry before regurgitating it to share with the world

  • The Protector of Asgard
  • Described as being huge and strong and having a red beard
  • His father is Odin, and his mother is Jord
  • He has a wife, Sif (known for her golden hair), and a stepson, Ullr
  • He has two sons, Modi and Magni, and a daughter, Thrud
  • Wears his belt of strength, Megingjord and iron gloves, which help him wield Mjollnir, a dwarf-forged hammer
  • Gaiman portrays him as having a rather "himbo"-like personality, where he is straightforward and occasionally somewhat dull or unaware, perhaps to contrast with Loki's subtlety and cunning
  • When Mjollnir is stolen, he crossdresses at Loki's behest to trick the giant Thrym, who sought Freya's hand in marriage in exchange for the return of Mjollnir

The Marvel Cinematic Universe

  • Full name: Odin Borson
  • King of Asgard
  • Portrayed (by Anthony Hopkins) as an old man with a short-to-medium-length beard, one eye (lost during the Battle of Tønsberg during the war against the Frost Giants)
  • Wields his spear, Gungnir, but the ring, Draupnir, does not appear
  • Starts off a ruthless and bloodthirsty warmonger alongside daughter Hela, Goddess of Death (portrayed by Cate Blanchett), but later decides to pursue peace across the Nine Worlds
  • Married to Frigga (a modern name change/feminization of Frigg that distances the name from the English word "frig" and its sexual connotations)
  • Has three children: Thor Odinson, Hela Odinsdottir, and Loki Laufeyson (adopted as an infant)
  • Dies in Norway of old age prior to Ragnarök and ascends to Valhalla (which is an astral plane that serves as an afterlife for Asgardians, since they are not truly immortal in this version)

  • Full name: Thor Odinson
  • Asgardian God of Thunder
  • Prince of Asgard and member of The Avengers
  • Portrayed (by Chris Hemsworth) as being very muscular, having long blond hair (and sometimes a beard), and with one electric blue eye after losing it in battle with his sister, Hela
  • His father is Odin Borson, and his mother is Frigga
  • He has a sister, Hela Odinsdottir, and an adoptive brother, Loki Laufeyson. He is often at odds with Loki, who seeks to rule Asgard, despite Thor being heir to the throne.
  • Was romantically involved with a human from Midgard, Jane Foster (portrayed by Natalie Portman), for a time, though they broke up after the Ultron Offensive.
  • Wields the magical hammer Mjølnir (which can only be lifted by one who is deemed worthy) and the enchanted ax Stormbreaker (after the destruction of Mjølnir)
  • Adopts Love, the daughter of Gorr the God-Butcher after Gorr's death

  • Full name: Loki Laufeyson (also as Loki Odinson in Thor: Ragnarok)
  • Asgardian God of Mischief
  • Biological son of Laufey, King of the Frost Giants
  • Was abandoned by his biological father not long after birth and adopted by Odin, who raised him as an Asgardian prince and brother of Thor
  • Was not told about his Frost Giant heritage until he sabotaged Thor's coronation by allowing Frost Giants into Asgard to attack
  • Led the Chitauri army in battle against the Avengers in New York City, but was ultimately defeated and imprisoned in Asgard.
  • Especially from Thor: Ragnarok onward, depicted more as an anti-hero than as a villain
  • Killed by Thanos at the beginning of Avengers: Infinity War

Conclusion

Overall, it is quite clear that Gaiman's Norse Mythology shares a vast amount of similarities to the original mythology. I would argue that this is to be expected, considering that it is merely intended as a retelling for modern audiences. The Marvel Cinematic Universe incarnations, however, take significantly more creative liberties when portraying these characters, resulting in a situation where the characters may have originally been based on the mythological figures, but have since been molded into entirely new chracters, with differing personalities, motives, and priorities.
